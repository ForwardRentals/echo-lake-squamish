Echo Falls. & the lake above it.

§ I  ·  The Headliner

The waterfall is the point. The lake is the bonus.

Almost everyone calls it the Echo Lake hike, because the trail ends at a lake. Almost everyone has it backwards. The payoff is halfway up. Echo Falls drops through a chute of dark Coast Mountains stone, loud enough to feel in your sternum, framed by old cedar and devil’s club. You hear it long before you see it. Stand in the spray on a warm afternoon and you understand why the trail exists.

Echo Lake itself is another push above the falls — a quiet alpine pocket tucked under the ridgeline, worth the swim if you’ve earned it and the bugs cooperate. Treat it as the appendix to the story, not the title. If your legs decide that the falls are enough for one day, the trip has already paid for itself.

“The falls are the real moment. The lake is the bonus. Plan the day around the waterfall and the rest of it sorts itself out.” House style, Echo Falls entry

The whole route lives on the west side of the Squamish River. There is no road in, no bridge nearby, no shortcut. To even reach the trailhead you have to cross moving water — which is where the planning for this trip actually lives.

§ II  ·  The Catch

A tidal river between you and the trailhead.

The Echo Falls trailhead sits on the west bank of the Squamish River, downstream of town. There is no bridge anywhere near it. You either paddle across in your own boat, or you hire one. That is the whole problem to solve before you ever lace up.

The good news: the Squamish is tidal this far up. The current flips with the tide, the channel is wide and slow when the water is in, and a properly timed crossing is more outing than ordeal. The less-good news: the launch and the landing are both intertidal mud, the water level swings by metres in a single day, and the river is real water with real consequences if you treat it casually.

Time the crossing for high tide. The river is meaningfully easier when the water is in.
§ III  ·  The Two Ways Across

Paddle yourself, or take the shuttle.

Two operations, same family, same trailhead. One delivers you a canoe and lets you handle the crossing yourself. The other handles the crossing for you, both ways. Pick the one that fits your group, your river comfort, and how much of the day you want to spend solving puzzles.

Option i.

Canoe, raft, or paddleboard — delivered.

Squamish Canoe Rental drops a vessel at the east-bank launch.

The classic option, and the cheaper one. A canoe or raft is staged at the launch ready to go. You paddle the channel, stash the boat in the trees on the far side, and start climbing. Suits paddlers who’ve been in a boat before and don’t mind a bit of tide math.

Tide

Time the river crossing for high tide. The channel is wider, slower, and far more forgiving.

Mooring

Pull canoes and rafts all the way up to the trees. Intertidal water swings metres; a boat parked at the waterline at low tide will be gone at high.

Paddles

Hide the paddles. They’ve been stolen before. Stash them in the brush, separate from the boat, and write it down.

Return

Coming back, float down to the culvert at the drop-off. Don’t paddle upstream — you’ll lose to the current and the daylight.

Raft Kit

If you’re on a raft, bring a hand pump. Slow leaks are routine, and finding out at the take-out is the worst time.

Signal

No cell service at the trailhead. Carry an offline map of the channel and tell someone your tide window before you launch.

§ V  ·  The Hike

Six kilometres up. The falls at half. The lake at the top.

From the landing, the trail climbs immediately and does not really stop. Nine hundred and fifty metres of gain over six kilometres puts the route firmly in serious-day-hike territory. Strong hikers compare it to climbing the Stawamus Chief twice, back to back, with a pack on. Most fit groups take three to three and a half hours to reach the lake. Markers are in place; route-finding is not really the problem.

The terrain is classic coastal rainforest: root steps, mossy boulders, the occasional steep dirt pitch that asks for both hands. You smell the falls before you see them. Above the falls the grade eases somewhat as the trail pushes toward the lake basin, and the canopy opens enough to remind you which valley you’re in.

Plan the day around the tide, not the trail. Cross at the high. Climb. Eat at the falls. Push up to the lake if the legs are willing. Hike down. Cross back. Be off the water before the light goes.

§ VI  ·  When To Go

A summer window. A morning tide. A weekday if you can.

The realistic season is late June to mid-September. Outside it, snow lingers on the upper trail and the falls are difficult to enjoy safely. Inside it, the deciding variable is the tide chart and the day of the week.

i.

Late June — July

Snowmelt swells the falls. The pool is loud and full and cold. Bugs in the upper forest can be aggressive; bring a head net if you’re sensitive. The river runs higher and the crossing window is more forgiving.

ii.

August — the sweet spot

Stable weather, warm enough to swim at the lake, the falls still running well. Weekends busy by Sea to Sky standards but never crowded. A weekday morning with a high tide near launch is the assignment.

iii.

Early September

Cool, clear, quiet. The river is at its lowest and slowest, the trail at its driest. Watch the long-range forecast: fall systems in the corridor can shut things down in twenty-four hours.

§ VII  ·  Honest Talk

The things that actually go wrong here.

No fearmongering, no listicles. These are the specific issues that come up on this specific trip, and why each deserves a quiet line in the plan.

  • i. The river has current. The Squamish is wide and tidal in this stretch, but it is still moving water with occasional debris and a flow direction that flips with the tide. Cross at high, wear the PFD, and do not try to muscle upstream on the way home.
  • ii. The landings are intertidal. Mud, slick rock, and a water level that can move metres in a few hours. Pull the boat all the way to the treeline, tie off if you can, and do not assume the spot you launched from will look the same when you return.
  • iii. The trail is steep, all of it. 950 metres of gain in 6 kilometres is not casual. The descent is harder on knees than the climb is on lungs. Trekking poles save the second half of the day.
  • iv. Coast Mountain weather flips fast. Clear morning, cold rain by mid-afternoon. Carry a shell and a warm layer even when the forecast looks perfect. The valley holds water; the upper forest stays cold.
  • v. Bear country, no cell service. Black bears live throughout this valley. Carry bear spray, make noise on the blind corners, and do not depend on a phone — there is no signal at the trailhead and very little along the trail.
  • vi. Rafts leak. Bring the pump. Slow leaks are normal across a full day in the sun. A small hand pump weighs almost nothing in a dry bag and rescues the return trip.
Bonus §  ·  While You’re Here

An estuary loop, for the way out.

If you’ve still got energy after the hike and you’re floating back in the canoe, there’s a quiet estuary loop you can paddle on the way to the take-out. Flat water, herons and eagles, the Tantalus on the horizon from a different angle. It’s a much gentler ending to the day than fighting current.

It also stands on its own as a paddle if the climb isn’t on the menu — worth a half-day in its own right.
